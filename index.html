1. The first two cards

The book's starting point: not every hand is playable, and the value of a hand depends on position, the number of players already in the pot, and how loose or tight the table is. Sklansky and Malmuth group starting hands into eight tiers by rough playability (see the full table below — derived earlier in this thread and re-verified against source).

1AA, AKs, KK, QQ, JJ
2AKo, AQs, AJs, KQs, TT
3AQo, ATs, KJs, QJs, JTs, 99
4AJo, KQo, KTs, QTs, J9s, T9s, 98s, 88
5A9s–A2s, KJo, QJo, JTo, Q9s, T8s, 97s, 87s, 77, 76s, 66
6ATo, KTo, QTo, J8s, 86s, 75s, 65s, 55, 54s

Insight: the group system is a starting filter, not a fixed strategy — the book is explicit that how many players are already in the pot shifts a hand's real value more than its group number does. A Group 5 suited connector goes up in value the more people see the flop with you (implied odds compound); a Group 1 big pair goes down in relative value in a very loose multiway pot (more live hands to outdraw it).

Chen formula (a numeric shortcut to the same ranking)

A quick point system for scoring any two starting cards, useful at the table without memorizing all 169 combos:

  1. High card score: A=10, K=8, Q=7, J=6, T=5, cards 9–2 = half their face value
  2. Pair: double the single-card score, minimum 5
  3. Suited: +2
  4. Gap between the two cards: 0 gap = no penalty, 1-gap = −1, 2-gap = −2, 3-gap = −4, 4+ gap = −5
  5. Straight bonus: +1 if gap ≤ 1 and the high card is Jack or lower
  6. Round up to the nearest whole number

Computed across all 169 starting combinations, the hands scoring 7–10 (roughly "worth entering a pot with in normal position"):

ScoreHands
10AKo, AJs, KQs, TT
9AQo, KJs, QJs, JTs, 99
8AJo, ATs, KQo, KTs, QTs, J9s, T9s, 98s, 88
7A9s, A8s, A7s, A6s, A5s, A4s, A3s, A2s, KJo, QJo, Q9s, JTo, T8s, 97s, 87s, 77, 76s
Insight: Chen scoring is a heuristic, not equity — it grades every suited-Ace (A2s through A9s) at exactly 7 points because it only sees "Ace + suited," even though A9s and A2s play very differently in practice (A9s makes top pair more often; A2s is mostly a flush/wheel-draw hand riding one big card). Treat identical Chen scores as "same starting tier," not "same hand."

2. Strategic concepts

The core chapters build a small set of ideas that recur through the rest of the book:

  • Position — acting later means more information; the same hand is playable in the cutoff/button that should be folded under the gun.
  • The free card — raising with a draw (semi-bluffing) can win the pot outright, or buy a check on a later street that lets you see another card for free if called.
  • Semi-bluffing — betting a hand that isn't the best right now but has outs to become the best; different from a pure bluff because it has two ways to win (fold equity + improve equity).
  • Inducing bluffs — sometimes checking a strong hand invites a weaker player to bet into you, extracting value they wouldn't have paid if you'd bet first.
  • Staying with a draw — a pot-odds/implied-odds calculation: is the money already in the pot (plus what you expect to win later) enough to justify chasing a flush or straight?
  • Playing when a pair flops — the book distinguishes top pair, middle pair, and bottom pair as different problems with different correct actions, not one category.
Insight: almost every "strategic concept" chapter is really the same question asked from a different angle — what does my opponent's range do in response to this action, and is that response profitable for me? The free card, the semi-bluff, and inducing a bluff are the same idea (shape the opponent's decision, don't just react to the board) applied at three different moments in a hand.

3. Playing in loose games

A large addition in the 21st Century Edition. The core adjustment: in a loose, multiway pot, hands that need to improve (suited connectors, small-to-mid pairs) go up in value because of implied odds from more callers, while big unpaired cards and one-pair hands go down in relative value because they're more likely to be up against a hand that outdraws them by the river.

Insight: this is the single biggest way the book's advice changes with game type — the same hand (say, KQo) can be a clear raise in a tight 6-max game and a marginal call-or-fold in an 8-way limped pot, because "how many live hands can beat me by the river" is doing more work than the two cards themselves.

4. Playing short-handed

Fewer players means wider ranges are correct everywhere — hands that are folds in a full ring game become opens, and position matters even more because there are fewer players left to act behind you. Aggression compounds: in short-handed play, betting/raising wins pots outright far more often simply because there are fewer opponents who can have a hand.

5. Other non-standard games, reading hands, psychology

The closing sections cover unusual structures (e.g. very wild/loose home-style games) and the softer skills: putting opponents on a range from their betting pattern, and adjusting to individual tendencies rather than playing a single fixed strategy against everyone.

Insight: the book's implicit thesis, restated in modern terms — starting-hand charts and the Chen formula are the training wheels; the actual skill the book is teaching is range-narrowing on every street based on position, pot size, and the specific player across from you. The numeric systems earlier in the book exist to free up attention for that harder skill, not to replace it.
